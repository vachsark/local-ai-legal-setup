# New Associate Guide

_For attorneys in their first 12 months at a law firm. This guide covers what the partner orientation doesn't._

---

> **About this guide**: Written for new associates — people who have a law degree, passed the bar, and now need to figure out how to actually practice law. The goal is to be honest, practical, and spare you the lessons that usually come from embarrassing mistakes.

> **AI limitation notice**: This guide was produced with AI assistance. Firm practices, billing norms, and procedural requirements vary by firm, practice area, and jurisdiction. When in doubt, ask your supervising attorney, not an AI.

## 1. First Day / First Week Checklist

### Before You Walk In

- [ ] Read your offer letter again — note your start date, salary, any signing bonus conditions
- [ ] Confirm bar admission status — you cannot practice until admitted; check your firm's policy on supervised work pending admission
- [ ] Review the employee handbook if provided in advance
- [ ] Set up your professional email signature with your bar admission state(s) and "Admission Pending" if not yet admitted

### Day One

- [ ] Get your building access, parking pass, and key card
- [ ] Set up voicemail with your name and a professional greeting
- [ ] Set up your work email — use it for all work communications, never personal email
- [ ] Locate the firm's document management system (DMS) and get a login
- [ ] Find out how to create new matter numbers (or who creates them for you)
- [ ] Identify your secretary/assistant and understand what tasks are routed through them
- [ ] Find the kitchen, printer, copy room, and conference room booking system
- [ ] Get the IT help desk number — you will need it
- [ ] Ask if there's a new associate orientation scheduled; if not, ask when you can meet with HR

### First Week

- [ ] Schedule brief intro meetings with every partner and senior associate you'll work with (20-30 minutes, their schedule)
- [ ] Read the firm's billing guidelines — not skimming, actually reading
- [ ] Find the firm's style guide for memos, briefs, and correspondence (if one exists)
- [ ] Ask about the firm's conflict check process — you must run one before doing any work on a matter
- [ ] Learn the docketing/calendaring system — missing deadlines ends careers
- [ ] Identify the form files and precedent documents for your practice area
- [ ] Ask how to access the legal research subscriptions (Westlaw, Lexis, etc.)
- [ ] Set up your bar association membership and confirm your CLE tracking system

### First Month

- [ ] Complete all onboarding paperwork
- [ ] Complete any required ethics/compliance trainings
- [ ] Submit your first time entries on time and in the firm's required format
- [ ] Have a one-on-one with your supervising partner or mentor — establish expectations
- [ ] Understand how work is assigned in your group: direct assignment, work coordinator, or open intake
- [ ] Read at least one recent brief or memo from your practice group to understand the writing style

## 2. Working with Partners and Senior Associates

### The Basic Dynamic

You are junior. That is not a criticism — it is the structure of how law firms work and how you will develop. The partners and senior associates know things you don't know yet. Your job for the first year is to make them look good, do excellent work, and learn everything you can.

### Getting Work

- **Most firms don't assign work automatically.** If you're not busy, you need to ask. "I have capacity for a research project or document review — do you have anything I could work on?"
- Ask more than one person. If you rely on a single partner for all your work, you have a single point of failure.
- When you ask for work, say your availability honestly: "I have about 20 hours available this week" is better than "I'm free" (implying you have nothing to do, which raises questions).

### Receiving Assignments

When you receive an assignment, clarify before you start — not in the middle and not after:

- **What is the deliverable?** A memo? An email? A draft motion? Bullet points?
- **What is the deadline?** "Soon" and "when you get to it" have caused many missed deadlines. Get a date. If it's a hard deadline (filing, statute of limitations), make sure you know that.
- **What is the scope?** How much time should this take? (If you're asked to do 4 hours of research and it's going to take 20, say so before you're 15 hours in.)
- **Who is the client?** So you can run a conflict check and bill correctly.
- **What does the partner already know?** Don't research from scratch what they've already analyzed.
- **What format do they prefer?** Some partners want a one-page email summary; others want a 20-page memo. Asking beats guessing.

### Delivering Work

- **Meet your deadline.** If you're going to miss it, say so at least 24 hours in advance — not the night before at 11 PM. Problems are manageable; surprises are not.
- **Come back with a recommendation.** Don't just say "here are the cases I found." Tell the partner what the cases say, what they mean for the matter, and what you think the best argument or approach is.
- **Flag problems early.** If your research reveals something bad for the client's position, the partner needs to know. Not telling them until the deadline is worse than the bad news itself.
- **Proofread.** Everything. Typos in a memo are irritating. Typos in a court filing are embarrassing. Typos in a client contract could be malpractice.

### Working with Senior Associates

Senior associates are your most useful resource. They know what the partners actually want, the firm's institutional knowledge, and the shortcuts that take years to learn. Treat them with the same respect you give partners.

- Ask them to review your work before you send it to the partner — most will say yes
- Ask them what mistakes new associates typically make in this practice group
- Ask what they wish someone had told them in their first year

### When a Partner Gives Feedback

- **Do not get defensive.** The partner is not attacking you; they are teaching you.
- **Ask questions when you don't understand the feedback.** "Can you show me an example of what you mean?" is always appropriate.
- **Fix it and resubmit.** Show that you can take feedback and improve. That's what the partner is watching for.
- **Do not make the same mistake twice.** Once is learning. Twice is a pattern.

## 3. Billing Practices and Time Entry

### The Core Obligation

Under ABA Model Rule 1.5, fees must be reasonable and communicated to the client in writing. Time entries are not just administrative records — they are the basis for a bill that the client will pay or dispute. Every entry needs to be accurate, appropriate, and describable to a client who might one day see it.

### Billing Basics

**Minimum increment**: Most firms bill in 0.1 hour (6-minute) increments. Round to the nearest 0.1 — do not artificially inflate.

**When to bill**: Enter time the same day. Memory degrades rapidly. Reconstructing a week of time on Friday afternoon produces inaccurate entries.

**What to bill**: Your time spent on client matters. This includes research, drafting, review, client calls, internal team calls about the matter, travel to/from court (if firm policy), and document review.

**What not to bill (typically)**:

- Learning the basics of an area of law you should already know (the learning curve is a write-off, not a client cost)
- Administrative tasks that are overhead (scheduling software issues, your own billing questions)
- Time that would embarrass you if a client saw it

### Writing Good Time Entries

A useful time entry answers: **What did I do? On what matter/issue? What was the result?**

**Too vague** (will get written off or questioned):

```
Research — 3.5 hours
```

**Good**:

```
Research re: statute of limitations applicable to breach of fiduciary duty claims
in California; analysis of discovery rule tolling and delayed discovery doctrine;
prepare research memorandum summarizing findings — 3.5 hours
```

**Overly detailed (unnecessary)**:

```
Read cases at pages 1-15 of Westlaw search results; opened Brown v. Smith,
read holding, noted this was not on point; opened next case... — 3.5 hours
```

### The Write-Off Reality

Partners regularly write off time from new associates — not because the work is wrong, but because:

- The matter budget doesn't support it
- A 1-hour task that took you 4 hours shouldn't cost the client for your learning
- The client relationship requires a reduction

**Record your time anyway.** Even if you suspect some will be written off, accurate time records are required. Selectively under-billing because you're embarrassed about the time it took is a worse problem than a write-off.

### Common Billing Mistakes

- **Block billing**: Lumping multiple tasks into one entry. ("Research, drafting, calls — 6.0 hours.") Many jurisdictions and clients require task-by-block separation.
- **Billing for the same task twice**: Reading a document, then billing for "review" of the same document separately.
- **Rounding up habitually**: Rounding 0.06 hours to 0.2 hours consistently is billing fraud.
- **Forgetting to open the matter**: Time entered on the wrong matter number or no matter number is a bookkeeping nightmare.

## 4. Client File Management

### The Golden Rule

**If it's not in the file, it didn't happen.**

Document everything: phone calls (date, who called, what was discussed, any decisions made), emails received (confirm you're saving them to the file), instructions from supervisors, research decisions, drafts and revisions.

### File Organization

Most firms use a document management system (DMS). Learn the firm's folder structure and naming conventions. Common structure:

```
Matter: [Client Name] - [Matter Description]
├── Correspondence/
│   ├── Incoming/
│   └── Outgoing/
├── Pleadings/         (litigation matters)
├── Discovery/         (litigation matters)
├── Contracts/         (transactional matters)
├── Due Diligence/     (transactional matters)
├── Research Memos/
├── Drafts/
│   └── [Document Name] v1, v2, v3...
└── Final/
```

### Document Naming

Use consistent naming. Many firms have conventions — learn and follow them. A general approach:

```
YYYY-MM-DD [Document Type] [Subject] [Version].docx
2026-03-21 Memo Re Jurisdiction Issue v2.docx
2026-03-21 Motion for Summary Judgment FINAL.docx
```

Never name a document "Final Final" or "Final_v3_REVISED_ACTUALFINAL." Use version numbers.

### Email Management

- **Save substantive emails to the file.** Most firms have a DMS email plugin. Use it.
- **Use subject lines with matter information**: "Smith v. Jones (Matter #12345) — Deposition Schedule"
- **Do not communicate client information via personal email.** Ever.
- **Reply to client emails within 24 hours**, even if only to say you received it and will respond fully by [date].

### Confidentiality

- Never discuss client matters in public places (elevators, restaurants, rideshares)
- Lock your screen when stepping away from your desk
- Shred or use secure disposal for any printed client documents
- If you lose a device with client data, report it to firm IT and supervising partner immediately

## 5. Research Workflow

### Start with Secondary Sources

New attorneys often make the mistake of jumping immediately to case law searches. Secondary sources (treatises, practice guides, legal encyclopedias, law review articles) give you:

- The doctrinal framework you need to know what you're looking for
- Validated starting points (cases and statutes the treatise cites are typically real and good law)
- The vocabulary to search effectively

For any new research area: find the relevant treatise or practice guide first.

### The Research Funnel

```
Secondary Sources (framework, vocabulary, starting citations)
        ↓
Statutes and Regulations (controlling text)
        ↓
Controlling Case Law (binding authority)
        ↓
Persuasive Authority (if gap in controlling law)
        ↓
Shepardize / KeyCite every case you plan to cite
```

### Search Strategies

**Natural language searching** works well for broad issue identification. "Can an employer terminate for off-duty social media posts?"

**Boolean searching** is more precise for finding cases with specific elements:

```
employ! /s terminat! /p "social media" /p "off duty"
```

**Build a search term list** before you start: the legal terms of art, synonyms, lay terms, and related concepts.

### Knowing When You're Done

Research saturation occurs when:

- Multiple independent search paths lead to the same cases
- Your secondary sources, case searches, and statute searches all converge on the same authority
- New searches are returning cases you've already reviewed
- You can state the rule and the supporting authority without looking it up

### Documenting Your Research

Keep a research log as you work:

- What databases you searched
- What search terms you used
- What you found and didn't find
- Cases you reviewed and why you did or didn't use them
- Gaps (issues where you found no controlling authority)

This log serves two purposes: it lets the partner see your methodology, and it protects you if the research is questioned later.

### Verify Before Citing

**Run KeyCite or Shepard's on every case you cite.** This is not optional. A red flag means the case may have been overruled — using it in a filing could be a Rule 3.3 (Candor) violation and malpractice risk.

## 6. Writing Standards

### The Purpose of Legal Writing

Legal writing exists to persuade or inform. It is not an opportunity to show off your vocabulary, demonstrate how much research you did, or explore every tangent. The reader — a partner, judge, opposing counsel, or client — is busy. Respect their time.

### Memo Structure

A legal memo has a specific structure. Follow it:

```
MEMORANDUM

TO:      [Name, Title]
FROM:    [Your Name]
DATE:    [Date]
RE:      [Matter Name/Number — Subject]

QUESTION PRESENTED
[One or two sentences stating the precise legal question. "Whether [party] is
liable for [act] under [standard] when [key facts]?"]

SHORT ANSWER
[1-3 sentences answering the question. Give the answer first, then the basis.
Do not make the reader read to page 6 to find out the answer.]

FACTS
[Relevant facts only. Organized chronologically or by issue. No legal conclusions
in the facts section.]

DISCUSSION
[IRAC analysis of each issue. One header per issue.]

CONCLUSION
[Brief summary of answers to each question presented. Recommend a course of action
if appropriate.]
```

### Writing Rules

**State your conclusion first.** Readers should not have to read to the end to know where you land. State the conclusion at the beginning of every section, then support it.

**One sentence, one idea.** If a sentence runs over three lines, it can almost always be broken into two sentences.

**Active voice, almost always.** "The court held that..." not "It was held by the court that..."

**Short paragraphs.** Long paragraphs are hard to read and hide the analysis.

**Headings and subheadings.** Long memos without headers are reader-hostile. Use them.

**No "I" in formal memos and briefs.** "This memo analyzes..." not "I will analyze..." (client letters are an exception)

**Proofread for:** spelling, grammar, citation format, internal cross-references, defined terms used consistently, party names consistent throughout.

### Common Writing Mistakes

- **Burying the conclusion**: Making the reader find the answer. Tell them first.
- **Block quoting everything**: Courts and partners skim block quotes. Quote the minimum necessary; explain what it means.
- **Citing string cites for simple propositions**: Five citations for "negligence has four elements" signals insecurity, not research skill.
- **Stating conclusions without analysis**: "Therefore, the defendant is liable" with no application of the rule to the facts is conclusory and earns no points.
- **Using "clearly," "obviously," "plainly"**: If it were obvious, you wouldn't need to write the memo.

## 7. Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the mistakes that follow attorneys for years — or end their careers early.

### Missing Deadlines

There is almost no mistake more serious in legal practice than a missed filing deadline. Statutes of limitations, court filing deadlines, and contractual deadlines can be outcome-determinative. Missing one is malpractice. Missing one you knew about is worse.

**Mitigation**: Calendar every deadline when you first learn about it. Calendar a reminder 2 weeks out and again 3 days out. Never rely on mental reminders alone. Learn your firm's docketing system on your first week and use it.

### Not Confirming Deadlines

Even if you believe you know the deadline, confirm it in the applicable rules. Court filing deadlines depend on local rules, standing orders, holiday schedules, and weekend adjustment rules. "I thought it was 30 days" is not a defense.

### Failing to Communicate

Clients and partners who don't hear from you assume bad things are happening. A brief "status update" email once a week on active matters — even if there's nothing new — is professional practice. It costs you 5 minutes; it prevents a lot of anxiety.

### Not Asking Questions Early

If you don't understand an assignment, ask before you start — not after 10 hours of work that misses the point. Experienced attorneys would rather clarify upfront than receive a memo that answered the wrong question.

The fear of looking dumb is real. It is also smaller than the cost of wasted work.

### Overconfidence in AI-Generated Research

AI tools can generate plausible-sounding citations that don't exist. If you use AI tools in your research, treat every citation as unverified until you confirm it yourself in Westlaw, Lexis, or the primary source. See the recent cases where attorneys have been sanctioned for filing briefs with AI-hallucinated citations.

### Sending Emails Without Rereading Them

The "reply all" accident. The attachment you forgot. The opposing counsel who is cc'd when they shouldn't be. The draft that went to the client instead of the partner. Read before sending.

### Treating Every Partner's Style as Universal

Different partners want different things. Some want a one-page email; others want a 30-page memo. Some want you to call with questions; others prefer you figure it out. Learn each person's preferences.

### Neglecting the Non-Billable Infrastructure

- **CLE**: Bar admission requires it. Don't get behind.
- **Conflict checks**: Run one on every new matter, every time. A conflict found late is a crisis; a conflict found early is a procedure.
- **Trust accounting**: If you handle client funds (retainers, settlements, cost advances), IOLTA rules are strict. A mistake here is a bar complaint.

### Talking About Clients Outside Secure Channels

Rule 1.6 is not a technicality. Client confidences are protected even if the information doesn't seem sensitive. Don't discuss matters on your commute, in restaurants, or in building common areas. Don't post about work on social media, even anonymously.

## 8. Professional Development Plan

### First 90 Days: Foundation

**Goal**: Learn the firm, do good work, establish yourself as reliable.

Skills to build:

- Legal research proficiency in your practice area's primary databases
- Your firm's document management, billing, and calendaring systems
- The formal and informal chain of command in your group
- The firm's writing style and formatting standards

Habits to establish:

- Same-day time entry
- Respond to emails within 24 hours
- Calendar every deadline the same day you learn about it
- Read every document before it leaves your hands

**Milestone**: At 90 days, you should be able to handle a basic research assignment from start to finish without hand-holding.

### Months 4-12: Developing Competence

**Goal**: Move from supervision-dependent to supervised-but-independent.

Skills to build:

- Drafting skills for your practice area's core documents (motions, contracts, memos)
- Courtroom or negotiation presence (take every opportunity to observe and participate)
- Client communication — supervised initially, then with more independence
- Understanding the economics of your matters (who is paying, what the budget is, what the stakes are)

**Seek out**:

- Pro bono matters where you can take more responsibility than you would on a client matter
- Opportunities to argue motions or attend depositions, even in a supporting role
- CLEs in your practice area (attend 2-3 in your first year beyond what's required)
- A mentor outside your immediate supervisor — someone who will give you candid career advice

**Milestone**: At 12 months, you should be able to draft the core work product of your practice area with relatively light edits from senior attorneys.

### Year Two and Beyond

**Goal**: Build expertise, expand your network, develop your own clients over time.

- Develop a specialty within your practice area
- Write (bar journal articles, client alerts, blog posts) — it builds reputation and forces you to learn
- Teach (CLE presentations, law school adjunct work) — teaching makes you master the material
- Build relationships outside the firm: opposing counsel, judges' clerks, industry contacts
- Understand the business development expectations for associates moving toward partnership

### CLE Planning

Most states require 12-15 CLE credits per year, including ethics credits. Check your state bar's requirements.

**Free and low-cost CLE options**:

- Your state bar's website (member discounts, free annual CLE)
- Law school alumni programming
- Bar association specialty sections
- Courtroom learning (attending hearings, observing trials)
- In-house firm trainings (usually count toward CLE)
- National Institute for Trial Advocacy (NITA) programs
- Webinar CLE providers (many have affordable bulk subscriptions)

## 9. Ethics Quick Reference

These are the situations most likely to arise in your first year. When in doubt, call your state bar's ethics hotline — most are free and confidential.

### Conflicts of Interest

Run a conflict check before doing any work on any matter. If a conflict is found:

- Stop work immediately
- Inform the supervising partner
- Do not discuss the matter further until the conflict is resolved or the matter is declined

**ABA Model Rule 1.7** governs current client conflicts. **Rule 1.9** governs former client conflicts.

### Confidentiality

Everything a client tells you in the course of representation is confidential — not just "secrets." The duty survives the representation, even if the client dies.

**ABA Model Rule 1.6** permits (but does not always require) disclosure to prevent substantial bodily harm or death, or to prevent or remedy certain frauds involving your legal services. These exceptions are narrow and jurisdiction-specific. Ask your supervising partner before disclosing anything.

### Candor to the Tribunal

**ABA Model Rule 3.3** requires you to:

- Never make a false statement of fact or law to a court
- Correct any prior false statement
- Disclose directly adverse controlling authority, even if opposing counsel didn't raise it
- Take reasonable measures to prevent a client from using you to commit a fraud on the court

If you discover your client lied in a filing, or that you cited a case that was overruled, you have an obligation to correct it.

### The Subordinate Attorney's Dilemma (Rule 5.2)

When a supervisor tells you to do something that makes you uncomfortable:

- You may follow a supervisor's reasonable resolution of an arguable question of professional conduct
- You may NOT follow instructions that clearly require a disciplinary rule violation
- "I was following my supervisor's instructions" is not a defense for a clear ethics violation

When uncertain: write down what you were asked to do and by whom. Raise your concern once, clearly. If the concern is serious, consult the firm's ethics partner, your state bar's ethics hotline, or personal ethics counsel.

### Trust Accounts

If your practice involves holding client funds (retainers, settlement proceeds, cost advances):

- Client funds must be kept in an IOLTA trust account, separate from firm operating funds
- Never use client funds for firm expenses, even temporarily
- Maintain meticulous records of deposits and disbursements
- **ABA Model Rule 1.15** governs this. Violations are among the most common causes of bar discipline.

## 10. When to Ask for Help

New attorneys are often reluctant to ask questions because they fear looking incompetent. This gets attorneys fired, disciplines, and sued.

**Always ask when:**

- You don't understand what you've been asked to do
- You've hit a deadline you can't meet
- You've discovered a problem with a matter (bad news never gets better with time)
- You're about to do something irreversible (file a document, make a representation to the court, settle a claim)
- You're uncertain whether something is ethically permissible

**The right way to ask:**

1. Do enough research that you can ask a specific question, not "can you explain everything about X to me?"
2. Come with your current understanding and your specific question
3. Propose a solution: "Here's what I was thinking of doing — does that make sense?"

**Who to ask:**

- Supervising partner first (it's their matter)
- Senior associate on the matter second
- Other senior associates for general "how does this work here" questions
- HR or office manager for firm policy questions
- Your state bar ethics hotline for ethics questions (free, confidential)
- Personal outside counsel for serious ethics situations (if you may personally be at risk)
