# New Associate: First 90 Days Checklist

_The practical checklist for your first three months. Everything the orientation doesn't cover._

> This checklist assumes you've passed the bar (or are pending admission under supervision) and are starting your first position at a law firm or legal organization. Adapt it to your employer's actual practices — when something on this list conflicts with your supervising attorney's instructions, follow the supervising attorney.

---

## Before You Start

- [ ] Re-read your offer letter — confirm start date, salary, any conditions on signing bonus
- [ ] Verify your bar admission status and timeline — you cannot practice until admitted; confirm your employer's policy on supervised work pending admission
- [ ] Research your employer: 5 recent cases or deals (for client conversations), the names of partners you'll work with, the firm's practice area focus
- [ ] Buy the right clothes now — not the week you start (business professional varies by market; when in doubt, wear a suit the first week)
- [ ] Set up a professional email signature template with your name, title, bar admission state(s), and "Admission Pending" note if applicable

---

## Week 1: Infrastructure

### Admin

- [ ] Building access, parking pass, key card
- [ ] Work email setup — **use only this email for work communications, never personal email**
- [ ] Voicemail setup with name and professional greeting (record it, then call yourself to check)
- [ ] Document management system (DMS) login and basic navigation
- [ ] IT help desk number saved in your phone
- [ ] Learn how to create or request new matter numbers
- [ ] Identify your assistant/secretary (if applicable) and understand what routes through them

### Learning the Rules

- [ ] Read the billing guidelines — actually read them, not skim (billing errors in year 1 follow you)
- [ ] Locate the docketing/calendaring system and get a walkthrough — **missing deadlines ends careers**
- [ ] Find the firm's style guide for memos, briefs, and correspondence (ask if you can't find it)
- [ ] Learn the conflict check process — run one before doing any work on any matter
- [ ] Confirm your access to Westlaw, Lexis, or the firm's primary research platforms
- [ ] Read one recent memo and one recent brief from your practice group — understand the house style

### People

- [ ] Schedule 20-minute intro meetings with every partner and senior associate you'll work with (this week or next — their schedule, not yours)
- [ ] Introduce yourself to the billing department, IT, and the conference room booking system
- [ ] Find out who the "go-to" senior associate is in your group — the one who answers first-year questions without judgment

---

## Weeks 2–4: Getting Work

### Taking Assignments

- [ ] When you receive an assignment, confirm before you start (not after):
  - What is the deliverable? (memo, email, bullet points, draft motion?)
  - What is the deadline? (get a date — "soon" has caused many missed deadlines)
  - How much time should this take? (if it's going to take longer, say so before you're 15 hours in)
  - Who is the client? (so you can run a conflict check and bill correctly)
  - What does the partner already know? (don't research what they've already analyzed)
- [ ] Submit your first time entries on time and in the firm's required format
- [ ] Ask a senior associate to review your first memo before it goes to the partner

### Observing

- [ ] Attend at least one deposition (even just to observe) if your practice area involves depositions
- [ ] Attend at least one court appearance or hearing if possible — sit in the gallery if you can't be counsel of record
- [ ] Ask to sit in on at least one client call — listening to how partners handle clients is invaluable
- [ ] Read the firm's or organization's standard engagement letter — understand what you're promising clients

### Habits to Build Now (easier to start right than to fix later)

- [ ] Enter time **the same day** — memory degrades rapidly; reconstructing a week on Friday is inaccurate
- [ ] Respond to emails within 24 hours, even if only to say you received it and will respond by [date]
- [ ] Calendar every deadline the same day you learn about it, with two reminders (2 weeks out and 3 days out)
- [ ] Save substantive emails to the matter file immediately
- [ ] Read everything before it leaves your hands — including the email "to" line

## Month 2: First Real Work

### Milestones

- [ ] Complete your first research assignment from start to finish
- [ ] Draft your first memo — have a senior associate review it before it goes to the partner
- [ ] Attend your first client call where you're introduced as counsel (not just an observer)
- [ ] Have a one-on-one check-in with your supervising partner to confirm you're meeting expectations
- [ ] Submit time entries on time for a full month (build the habit before it becomes a problem)

### Skills to Actively Practice

- [ ] **Billing descriptions**: Go back and look at your time entries from week 1. Would a client understand them? Are they specific? Are any of them block billing? Fix them before they're billed.
- [ ] **Research methodology**: Are you starting with secondary sources or jumping straight to case law? Secondary sources first — they orient you and give you search terms.
- [ ] **Email communication**: Before you send any email to a client or opposing counsel, ask: Is this what I'd want the partner to read over my shoulder? Would I be comfortable if this got forwarded?
- [ ] **Asking for feedback**: After you turn in a project, ask your supervising attorney what you did well and what to do differently next time. Most won't offer this unprompted.

### Professional Development

- [ ] Confirm your CLE requirements for the year and schedule your first CLE program
- [ ] Set up your bar association membership (most have free or discounted first-year rates)
- [ ] Identify one area of your practice where you want to build deeper expertise — start reading
- [ ] Find a mentor outside your immediate supervisor — someone who will give you candid career advice

---

## Month 3: Gaining Independence

### Milestones

- [ ] Draft a document (contract, motion, brief) that goes out with relatively light partner edits
- [ ] Handle at least one client communication independently (with partner review but not drafting)
- [ ] Argue a motion or appear in court in a supporting role (if litigation practice)
- [ ] Complete a pro bono matter from start to finish — pro bono is where first-years get responsibility faster

### Self-Assessment Questions

At the end of Month 3, honestly assess:

- [ ] Can I handle a standard research assignment from start to finish without asking for help at every step?
- [ ] Am I entering time every day, accurately?
- [ ] Have I made a mistake and disclosed it appropriately? (If not, you may have gotten lucky — have the habit ready)
- [ ] Do I understand who to ask for what? (Partner vs. senior associate vs. paralegal vs. IT)
- [ ] Have I established a relationship with at least 2 partners who know my name and work quality?
- [ ] Do I have a plan for my CLEs this year?

## Ongoing: The Right Habits

These are the habits that separate attorneys who advance from those who don't. Build them in the first 90 days.

### Communication

- **Update clients proactively** — a brief status email once a week on active matters costs 5 minutes and prevents a lot of anxiety
- **Tell partners bad news early** — problems are manageable; surprises are not
- **Never go silent** — if something is taking longer than expected, say so before the deadline, not after

### Work Product

- **Proofread everything** — typos in a memo are irritating; typos in a court filing are embarrassing; typos in a client contract could be malpractice
- **State your conclusion first** — partners skim; put the answer at the top, then explain it
- **Come with a recommendation** — don't just present research; tell them what the research means and what you think should happen

### Professional Judgment

- **Run conflict checks on every new matter** — every time, without exception
- **When you're about to do something irreversible, pause** — file a document, make a representation to the court, send an email you can't recall — ask yourself: did I confirm this with the supervising attorney?
- **If it's ethically unclear, ask** — your state bar has a free ethics hotline; the firm has an ethics partner; using them is not a sign of weakness

### When Things Go Wrong

- **Disclose mistakes immediately** — every attorney has them; the response determines the outcome
- **Tell the partner before you try to fix it yourself** — they need to know and may have authority you don't
- **Never cover up** — the cover-up is almost always worse than the mistake

## Quick Reference: Who to Ask for What

| Question                                     | Who to Ask                                      |
| -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Specific assignment scope or deadline        | Supervising partner                             |
| How this type of work is typically done here | Senior associate                                |
| Billing questions                            | Billing partner or firm administrator           |
| Research platform access                     | IT or research librarian                        |
| Ethics question                              | Firm ethics partner or state bar ethics hotline |
| Career advice                                | Mentor outside your supervising chain           |
| HR/benefits/policy                           | HR department                                   |
| Personal ethics risk                         | Personal outside ethics counsel                 |

## CLE Quick Reference

Most states require **12-15 CLE hours per year**, including ethics hours. Check your state bar.

**Free/low-cost options**:

- State bar website (member discounts, often free annual CLE programs)
- Law school alumni programs (often free)
- Bar association specialty sections
- In-house firm training programs (usually count toward CLE)
- Attending hearings or trials (some states count courtroom observation)
- Webinar providers (affordable bulk subscriptions)

**Track your CLEs from day one** — getting behind is an unnecessary stress and, in some states, can affect your license.
