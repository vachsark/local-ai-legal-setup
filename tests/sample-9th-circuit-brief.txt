JURISDICTIONAL STATEMENT

The district court had diversity jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1332.
This Court has jurisdiction over this appeal under 28 U.S.C. § 1291. The
district court entered a final judgment on October 15, 2024. This appeal
was timely filed on November 12, 2024.


STATEMENT OF ISSUES

1. Whether the district court erred in granting summary judgment when
   genuine issues of material fact existed regarding the breach of contract
   claim.

2. Whether Appellant is entitled to injunctive relief to prevent ongoing
   harm to its business operations.


STATEMENT OF THE CASE

This appeal arises from a breach of contract dispute between Acme
Corporation ("Acme" or "Appellant") and Global Tech LLC ("Global Tech" or
"Appellee"). Acme and Global Tech entered into a software licensing
agreement in January 2023 (the "Agreement"). Under the Agreement, Global
Tech was required to provide exclusive access to its proprietary software
platform and to maintain service availability of at least 99.5%.

Global Tech repeatedly failed to meet its service obligations, resulting
in significant downtime and financial losses for Acme. Specifically, Global
Tech's platform experienced seven outages exceeding four hours each between
March and September 2023, causing Acme to lose substantial business
opportunities. Acme notified Global Tech of each breach in writing and
demanded cure within thirty days as required by the Agreement.

Despite multiple notices, Global Tech failed to cure the breaches. Acme
filed suit in the Central District of California in October 2023, asserting
claims for breach of contract, breach of the implied covenant of good faith
and fair dealing, and injunctive relief.

The district court granted Global Tech's motion for summary judgment on
March 1, 2024, holding that Acme had failed to demonstrate damages with
sufficient certainty. This appeal followed.


SUMMARY OF ARGUMENT

The district court erred in two fundamental respects. First, the court
improperly weighed competing declarations and resolved factual disputes in
Appellee's favor, which is the exclusive province of the jury under Ninth
Circuit law. Second, the court applied the wrong legal standard to Acme's
request for injunctive relief, ignoring the Winter factors entirely.

Reversal is required because genuine issues of material fact preclude
summary judgment, and because Acme plainly satisfies the requirements for
injunctive relief.


ARGUMENT

I. THE DISTRICT COURT ERRED IN GRANTING SUMMARY JUDGMENT.

A. The Standard of Review Is De Novo.

This Court reviews a district court's grant of summary judgment de novo.
See Celotex Corp. v. Catrett, 477 U.S. 317, 322 (1986). Summary judgment
is appropriate only where "there is no genuine dispute as to any material
fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law." Fed. R.
Civ. P. 56(a). All inferences must be drawn in favor of the non-moving
party. See Anderson v. Liberty Lobby, Inc., 477 U.S. 242, 255 (1986);
Matsushita Elec. Indus. Co. v. Zenith Radio Corp., 475 U.S. 574, 587
(1986).

B. Genuine Issues of Material Fact Preclude Summary Judgment.

The district court committed reversible error by resolving disputed
factual questions against Appellant. The core dispute — whether Global
Tech's outages caused measurable harm to Acme's business — is a question
of fact for the jury. Acme submitted a detailed expert declaration from Dr.
Robert Chen, an economist, who calculated damages of approximately $2.3
million based on lost contracts and increased operational costs. The
district court dismissed Dr. Chen's analysis as "speculative," but this
assessment itself requires the weighing of competing evidence that is
improper at the summary judgment stage.

The Ninth Circuit has made clear that "credibility determinations, the
weighing of the evidence, and the drawing of legitimate inferences from the
facts are jury functions, not those of a judge." Anderson v. Liberty Lobby,
477 U.S. at 255. The district court's dismissal of Dr. Chen's declaration
violates this basic principle.


II. APPELLANT IS ENTITLED TO INJUNCTIVE RELIEF.

To obtain a permanent injunction, a plaintiff must demonstrate: (1)
success on the merits; (2) that it would suffer irreparable harm absent the
injunction; (3) that the balance of equities tips in its favor; and (4)
that the public interest would not be disserved. See eBay Inc. v.
MercExchange, L.L.C., 547 U.S. 388, 391 (2006); see also Winter v.
Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 555 U.S. 7, 20 (2008).

Acme satisfies each of these requirements. Because we seek reversal of
summary judgment, the merits question is preserved for the jury. As to
irreparable harm, Acme's expert testified that each additional month of
service disruption risks permanent loss of at least three anchor clients,
harm that monetary damages cannot fully compensate. The balance of equities
clearly favors Acme, as Global Tech need only fulfill its existing
contractual obligations. And the public interest supports enforcement of
commercial contracts.


CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Appellant Acme Corporation respectfully requests
that this Court reverse the district court's grant of summary judgment and
remand this matter for further proceedings, and grant the injunctive relief
requested.
