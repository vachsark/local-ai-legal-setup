INTRODUCTION

Defendant Northstar Capital Group Inc. ("Northstar") moves to dismiss the Complaint filed
by Plaintiff Meridian Partners LLC ("Meridian") for failure to state a claim upon which
relief can be granted. Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(6). Meridian's breach of contract claim fails
as a matter of law because the parties' agreement expressly allocated the very risk Meridian
now seeks to shift to Northstar. Its tortious interference claim fares no better — it rests
on wholly conclusory allegations that cannot survive Iqbal/Twombly scrutiny.


BACKGROUND

Meridian and Northstar entered into a joint venture agreement in February 2023 to develop
commercial real estate in lower Manhattan. The agreement contained an express force majeure
clause that excused Northstar's performance obligations "in the event of any circumstances
beyond Northstar's reasonable control, including but not limited to market disruptions,
regulatory changes, or adverse economic conditions." Compl. ¶ 12.

In October 2023, Northstar invoked the force majeure clause following a sharp increase in
construction costs attributable to supply chain disruptions and revised zoning requirements
by the New York City Department of Buildings. Meridian contends this invocation was
improper and sues for breach of contract and tortious interference with Meridian's
anticipated business relationships with third-party contractors.

Northstar now moves to dismiss.


ARGUMENT

I. MERIDIAN'S BREACH OF CONTRACT CLAIM FAILS BECAUSE THE FORCE MAJEURE CLAUSE
   EXPRESSLY COVERS THE CIRCUMSTANCES AT ISSUE.

Under New York law, force majeure clauses are enforced according to their plain terms.
See Kel Kim Corp. v. Central Markets, Inc., 70 N.Y.2d 900, 902 (1987). A party seeking
to invoke a force majeure clause need only demonstrate that the triggering conditions
specified in the contract have occurred. See id.

Here, the force majeure clause expressly covers "market disruptions" and "regulatory
changes." The supply chain disruptions and the zoning changes from the Department of
Buildings are precisely the types of events the parties contemplated and allocated when
they negotiated the agreement. Meridian cannot now seek to undo that allocation simply
because the clause operates in Northstar's favor.

Meridian's contrary argument — that force majeure clauses require the party invoking them
to demonstrate complete impossibility — is flatly inconsistent with New York law. See 407
East 61st Garage, Inc. v. Savoy Fifth Ave. Corp., 23 N.Y.2d 275, 281 (1968) (force
majeure clause governs when it "specifically covers" the event at issue).

II. MERIDIAN'S TORTIOUS INTERFERENCE CLAIM IS FATALLY CONCLUSORY.

To state a claim for tortious interference with prospective economic advantage under New
York law, a plaintiff must allege: (1) a business relationship with a third party;
(2) the defendant's knowledge of that relationship; (3) intentional interference with the
relationship by improper means; and (4) damages. See Carvel Corp. v. Noonan, 3 N.Y.3d
182, 189-90 (2004).

Meridian's tortious interference claim fails at the threshold because the Complaint does
not identify a single specific business relationship that Northstar allegedly disrupted.
Instead, Meridian refers only to vague "anticipated relationships with third-party
contractors." Compl. ¶ 28. This is precisely the type of conclusory allegation that the
Supreme Court rejected in Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662, 678 (2009) and Bell Atl.
Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544, 570 (2007). A complaint must plead "enough facts to
state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face." Twombly, 550 U.S. at 570. The
Complaint here falls well short.

Moreover, Meridian fails to allege that Northstar acted by "improper means" — a distinct
element under New York law that requires conduct that is independently tortious or in
violation of a recognized duty. See Carvel Corp., 3 N.Y.3d at 190. Northstar simply
exercised its contractual rights. That is not tortious interference.


CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant Northstar Capital Group Inc. respectfully requests
that the Court dismiss the Complaint in its entirety with prejudice.
